Microscopy and Microbiology Checklist

  • Distinguish between a simple and a compound microscope.
  • Calculate the total magnification of a compound microscope.
  • Explain how immersion oil increases resolution.
  • Describe relationships between resolving power, numerical aperture, and wavelength.
  • Explain why a blue filter is used with light bulb microscopes.
  • Describe changes in working distance as magnification increases.
  • Explain parfocality and its advantages.
  • Locate and name microscope parts with their functions.
  • List rules for proper care and storage of a microscope.
  • Describe changes in the field of view with magnification.
  • Recognize how microscope optics change image orientation.
  • Identify and describe bacterial shape and arrangement using oil immersion.
  • Properly clean the optical components of a microscope.
  • Compare sizes of blood cells, yeast, bacilli, and cocci.
  • Define "depth of field" and observe it with slides.
  • Define pure culture, mixed culture, aseptic, sterile, contamination, etc.
  • Identify times when pure cultures are necessary.
  • Identify characteristics of a properly adjusted Bunsen burner flame.
  • Describe instruments used to transfer microorganisms.
  • Differentiate serological and Mohr pipettes.
  • Understand how to read volume on a pipette.
  • Identify aseptic techniques before, during, and after microbiology work.
  • Demonstrate precautions when handling bacterial cultures.
  • Practice aseptic technique with loops, needles, broths, and slants.
  • Explain how to prevent laboratory-acquired infections.
  • Understand the purpose and handling of stock cultures.
  • Identify common uses for poured plates.
  • Prepare and store stock cultures.
  • Prepare sterile plates and understand their incubation.
  • Identify contamination sources in plated media.
  • Differentiate mixed and pure cultures.
  • Explain why pure cultures are needed.
  • Understand why plates are labeled on the bottom and inverted.
  • Define concentration gradient, colony, CFU, etc.
  • Describe streak plate and spread plate methods.
  • Avoid contamination when preparing plates.
  • Identify factors affecting colony size.
  • Describe microbial morphology characteristics.
  • Define cation, anion, chromogen, chromophore, and auxochrome.
  • Differentiate basic and acidic dyes.
  • Prepare bacterial smears from liquid and solid media.
  • Understand air-drying and heat-fixing of slides.
  • Use positive staining techniques.
  • Differentiate positive and negative stains.
  • Identify two common negative stains.
  • Explain how negative stains interact with bacteria.
  • Observe and describe bacteria using negative stains.
  • Understand why negative stains are not heat fixed.
  • Identify reasons for using negative stains.
  • Identify and stain bacterial capsules.
  • Summarize the history and importance of Gram staining.
  • Identify the reagents used in Gram staining.
  • Compare cell wall structures of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria.
  • Identify Gram-nonreactive bacteria and their characteristics.
  • Recognize factors that affect Gram stain results.
  • Practice and interpret Gram stains effectively.
